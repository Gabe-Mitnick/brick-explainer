<step>
Imagine a brick wall.
<substep>
You're probably picturing something like this:

[bricks animate falling from above to form stretcher bond]

<step>
Each *course*, or vertical layer, is made of a row of bricks laid end-to-end.
[courses glow one by one]
<substep>
Each brick laid in this orientation is called a *stretcher*. Because of this, this pattern is called *stretcher bond*.
[bricks the first course glow one by one, then the second course, then the third course, etc.]

<step>
Stretcher bond is often used in modern buildings as a facade. Behind the facade is a structural wall made of wood or reinforced concrete. That's what really holds the building up.
[camera turns to reveal concrete structural wall]

<step>
In between the bricks and the structural wall, there's often a gap, sometimes filled with insulation or sometimes left empty. The gap provides moisture control and insulation. This is called a *cavity wall*.
[bricks become transparent and shift away from the wall to create a gap]
<substep>
The bricks are connectd to the structural wall with metal ties.
[metal ties appear between the bricks and the wall]

<step>
But in traditional masonry, there were no cavities, no ties, and no reinforced concrete. A brick wall was really a brick wall.
[concrete structural wall and wall ties disappear, leaving just the brick wall]
<substep>
And a stretcher bond wall like this is too thin to stand on its own.
[wall collapses]

<step>
To make a strong brick wall, you need multiple vertical layers, called *wythes*. You might start by building 2 wythes of stretchers right next to each other.
[a stretcher bond wall is built from falling bricks; then another wythe is built in front of it in the same way]

<substep>
But these 2 wythes are only held together by mortar, so they'll split apart.
[the two wythes split apart, with the back one falling backward and the front one falling forward]

<step>
After a few courses of stretchers, we'll need something to bond the two wythes together.
[3 courses of 2 wythes of stretchers are built again]
<substep>
We can solve this problem with more bricks! Let's try rotating some of our bricks so they lay across the two wythes, with their heads visible on the faces of the wall. These bricks are called *headers*.
[a course of headers are added to the wall, laying across the two wythes]

<step>
Sometimes, when the bricks are being fired, the heads are glazed or burnt. That lets the headers stand out.
[headers turn darker - not all at once, but in a wave going across the wall. for the rest of the steps, headers are this darker color.]

<step>
There are different ways to arrange the headers that bond together the wythes.
[bricks fade]
<substep>
These different patterns are called *bonds*. Let's learn about some of them!
[no graphic]

<step>
If a wall has a few courses of stretchers for each course of headers, it's called *American bond*. In America, this is also called *Common bond*.
[american bond with 6 courses of stretchers, 1 course of headers, 6 course of stretchers, 1 course of headers, etc. ]
<substep>
This pattern is easy to build, but it's not very pretty.

<step>
If there's just 1 course of stretchers for each course of headers, it's called *English bond*. In England, this is also called *Common bond*.
[english bond with 1 course of stretchers, 1 course of headers, 1 course of stretchers, 1 course of headers, etc. ]
<substep>
People say this is one of the strongest bonds, but it looks very menacing to me.

<step>
Let's try offsetting every other course of stretchers by half a brick.
[every 4th course shifts to the right by half a brick]
<substep>
Much prettier! Look how the mortar joints between the bricks form diagonal lines.
[mortar joints are highlighted in a diagonal pattern going down and to the right, and then in a diagonal pattern going up and to the right]
<substep>
This is called *English cross bond* or *Dutch bond*, depending on how the ends of the wall are finished.

<step>
We don't have to use just stretchers or just headers in each course. What if we alternate them?
[2-wythe wall is formed of courses of alternating stretchers and headers]
<substep>
This is called *Flemish bond*. It's my favorite!

<step>
If each course has 2 stretchers for each header, it's called *Monk bond*.
[Stretchers fade, headers shift to the right and left, 2 stretchers appear in each gap where 1 stretcher was before]
